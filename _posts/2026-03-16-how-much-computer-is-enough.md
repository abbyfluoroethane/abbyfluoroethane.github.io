## *pls stop criticizing my favorite computer, it makes him sad :c*

Apple released MacBook Neo on March 11, and it might be the most fun product launch I've seen in years. I was at an Apple Store with my girlfriend near close and the tables with Neo were swarmed with people. We eventually squeezed in and both instantly fell in love. The size! The price! The colors! ***<span style="color: pink">Pink!!!</span>*** It really seemed like the perfect laptop, and we couldn't get enough of it. Riley and I ended up back at the Apple Store every day for the next four days, and we finally bought a Neo last night. Dude, fuck yes.

Somewhere along the way computers became boring, just tools to do work. It's easy to get caught up in raw specs, benchmarks, and optimization—especially in the tech sphere. MacBook Neo feels like Apple remembering that most people (me included!) just want something that feels good to use. It's something Apple got right years ago with the base MacBook, a laptop that in its final iteration was my favorite Apple design—until now. Between the colors, the size, and the more rounded edges compared to its contemporaries, Neo really feels like a callback to the laptops I fell in love with in elementary school.

![MacBook 2010]({{site.baseurl}}/assets/images/macbook-unibody.jpg)
![Macbook Neo]({{site.baseurl}}/assets/images/macbook-neo.jpg)

On top of style points, MacBook Neo is Apple's cheapest laptop ever—a $599 fanless 13-inch machine running the A18 Pro chip straight out of the iPhone 16 Pro, 8GB of unified memory, and a color accurate (98% sRGB) display. It's the first Mac to run an A-series chip instead of M-series—as long as you ignore [this fuckin' thing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developer_Transition_Kit#Apple_Silicon_Developer_Transition_Kit_(2020))—and it starts at $499 for students. It replaces both my Framework 13 and my 2015 MacBook Pro, which means I consolidated two aging and unreliable laptops into one tiny $500 machine that smokes both of them. This thing flies compared to its predecessors!

8GB of RAM sounds rough in 2026—and honestly it can be—but macOS is genuinely good at memory management, and unified memory stretches further than the number suggests. Every review of this thing has the same conclusion: reviewers open a dozen apps, expect it to choke, and it just doesn't. The gap between what tech people stress test and what humans actually do with a laptop is enormous. For 90% of computer users, this machine isn't a compromise. It's overkill.

I'm not in that 90%, and it's still been great. I do graphic design, web and app development, and CAD—sometimes all in the same session. VS Code, the Affinity suite, Onshape, and Fusion have all run without complaint. As I write this in Obsidian I have Claude Code running in the background, a few documents open in Affinity Designer, a livestream playing in the background, and both Fusion and Onshape open. Sure, it's only been 24 hours, but i have yet to run into an issue where just moving stuff into swap has been a massive performance bottleneck. Coming from the machines I was on before, everything about this thing feels fast.

To be fair, Neo does come with some tradeoffs. No Force Touch trackpad: the mechanical click is fine, you won't care. sRGB instead of P3 wide color: unless you're doing color-critical print work, you will literally never notice. No keyboard backlighting: genuinely annoying, this is the one cut that actually stings for me. It's slightly thicker than the Air, who cares. The port situation is worth knowing about: you get two ports, but one is USB 3 and the other is USB 2.0, and they look identical. No Thunderbolt, single external display at 4K/60. For my use, none of this is a problem.

The real tradeoff is the 8GB ceiling with no upgrade path. That's not a limitation today, but it is a question about three years from now. The good news is that iFixit gave this thing a 6 out of 10 for repairability, the best score for a MacBook in 14 years. The battery sits in a tray on screws instead of adhesive. RAM and storage are still soldered, but that's a technological limitation and it's nice to see Apple moving in the right direction on a machine that a lot of first-time owners are going to hold onto for a while.

This isn't the best computer for everybody, but the best computer is the one you enjoy using. If you genuinely need—or just enjoy compensating with—a powerful computer, you have every right to buy one. But it's worth being honest with yourself about what you actually need, because wasted performance is just money down the drain. MacBook Neo is enough computer for almost everyone, and more importantly, it's the most fun I've had with a laptop in a long time. Computers should be fun. This one is.
