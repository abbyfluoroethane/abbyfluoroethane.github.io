When someone asks me what I do for work and I want to keep it vague, I tell them I work in defense. It's the truth — but sometimes the truth isn't what you want it to be.

I build rockets. Orbital rockets, not missiles — and that distinction matters to me, even if it's a blurry one. The hardware I put my hands on launches satellites that improve life on Earth, Moon landers, probes studying the cosmos. That's the version of this job I fell in love with. When I watched hardware I built go to space for the first time in January, barely a month into the job, it felt like magic. It still feels like magic.

But magic doesn't erase morality.

The Department of Defense is probably our largest customer. Whether it's an early-warning missile defense satellite for the Space Force or a [REDACTED] for the National Reconnaissance Office, these are tools of war that my beloved "peaceful" rocket launches. Same rocket, different payload, different customer — and suddenly a technology of wonder becomes a technology of war. The lines between NASA, commercial space, and the DoD aren't lines at all. They're gradients.

I dropped out of my aerospace engineering degree two and a half years ago because I couldn't stomach this exact problem — that the technology I'd be developing could land on the Moon or take the life of another person. And yet here I am, in the industry anyway. Crawling back not as an engineer, but as a technician. I didn't come back because I made peace with the dual-use problem. I came back because I want to further our reach into the cosmos, and because this job lets me put my hands on the machines that leave Earth.

I have friends and coworkers that work on things they can't talk about. We launch from a Space Force base. I'm all for strengthening the might of the U.S. military when its resources are helping Ukraine target Russian assets on occupied land. But when kids in Gaza are being turned to pink mist by airstrikes, it's hard not to feel complicit in something monstrous.

Space isn't neutral. It's political, strategic, violent. The same boosters that launch telescopes launch targeting systems. The same companies bidding for NASA science missions are bidding for Pentagon contracts. I'm part of that ecosystem whether I like it or not, and there are days I'm not sure where my values fall along that gradient.

I don't have a tidy moral calculus that tells me it's okay to keep doing what I do. I believe in space — not in some capitalist "space economy" kind of way, but in the old-school dreamer sense. I want us to explore, to learn, to maybe one day stand on another planet not as conquerors but as caretakers. I still believe that. And I still build the rockets. And I still don't know how to hold both of those things at the same time.

Maybe that's the point. Maybe there isn't a resolution, and the best I can do is refuse to look away from the discomfort. To name it, even when I can't fix it.

I'm naming it.
