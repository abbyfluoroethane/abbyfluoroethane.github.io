## _"FUTUUURE... FUTUUURE... FUTUUURE..." - Squidward Tentacles_

On Friday, February 27 at exactly 5:01 p.m. the Department of Defense—err, _Department of War_—did something truly unprecedented. Secretary of War Pete Hegseth announced on X, formerly known as Twitter, that Anthropic, an American AI lab at the top of the industry, and its flagship model, Claude, had been designated a supply chain risk after a week of tense, and public, contract negotiations over restrictions the lab imposes on Claude's capabilities. Their offense? Not allowing the DoW to use Claude for mass surveillance of Americans or for autonomous weapons.

The kicker of the whole situation is that Claude is already deeply integrated into the nation's military. Anthropic was the first AI company on classified military networks. Claude was used to help capture Venezuelan president Nicolas Maduro, and has been used over the weekend to assist U.S. forces in Iran and the Middle East during Operation Epic Fury. This is not a pacifist company.

They drew two lines: no mass surveillance of Americans, no fully autonomous weapons without human oversight. For that, the government branded them a national security threat—a label previously reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei. Federal agencies now have six months to rip Claude out of their systems and field a replacement.

Contemporary AI systems—Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and others—are far from ready to be anywhere near a kill chain or be piloting autonomous weapons. I would know, both at work and personally I use Claude extensively for coding, writing documentation, and data analysis. These are useful tools, but they are still extremely flawed and unreliable in ways most people don't appreciate. In the space launch industry we don't fly hardware that hasn't been tested to failure—AI hasn't passed that bar yet. Ask any of these models to give you a list of sources and half the links will be fabricated. These systems confidently invent information that doesn't exist. That's a nuisance when you're writing a report—it's a catastrophe when it's picking targets. 

Perhaps the government is so focused on "beating China" in AI adoption that it isn't ready to swallow that pill yet, **even if moving too fast will get people killed.**

When two parties can't agree on contract terms, the normal response is to walk away and find another vendor. Instead, the DoW threatened the Defense Production Act, or DPA, (a Korean War-era law intended for manufacturing) and designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk." As Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, pointed out: these two threats are inherently contradictory. One says Anthropic is a security risk, the other says Claude is essential to national security. **Claude already is critical to national security.** The AI model is used every day by our military and intelligence community and has been for nearly two years.

Over 450 employees at Google and OpenAI signed an open letter supporting Anthropic's position. Even Sam Altman—CEO of Anthropic's direct competitor, OpenAI—said the Pentagon shouldn't be threatening AI companies with the DPA.

Hours after Anthropic was blacklisted, OpenAI—the AI company behind ChatGPT and Sora—struck a deal with the Pentagon that includes essentially the same safeguards Anthropic was asking for, at least in words. No mass surveillance. No autonomous weapons. Human responsibility for use of force. As part of these negotiations OpenAI even asked the DoW to offer the same terms to all AI companies, including Anthropic. The question remains how "solid" those restrictions are. Anthropic bakes them into the model itself. Does OpenAI? Or are they just taking the federal government at their word? Surely taking the feds at their word has never backfired for anyone before.

This childish temper tantrum by Pete Hegseth, Donald Trump, and the Department of War was never about the safeguards. If it were, the OpenAI deal would have been rejected too. This is about sending a message to every American tech company: accept whatever terms the government demands, or face retaliation. That's not how a free market works. That's not how contract negotiation works. Using state power to punish a company for *negotiating contract terms* is the kind of coercion and state control of private industry the administration claims to oppose. Frankly un-American if you ask me.

Congress hasn't legislated rules for military AI. As Lawfare noted, this fight exists because the rules don't. A Cold War-era production statute is being used to resolve questions about autonomous weapons. That should concern everyone regardless of political alignment. The Pentagon says it has "no interest" in mass surveillance or autonomous weapons. If that's true, then putting it in writing should cost nothing. The fact that they're willing to blacklist an American company rather than accept two contractual carve-outs tells you something. This sets the precedent that any company negotiating with the federal government can be punished for declining terms. Not for failing to deliver, not for poor performance, but for having ethical standards the government finds inconvenient.

The people who build the hardware that keeps warfighters alive understand that "not ready" isn't disloyalty, it's upholding standards. We should demand the same standard from the software and celebrate, not punish, the companies with enough backbone to stand up to pressure.
